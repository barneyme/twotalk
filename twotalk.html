Two Talk

Two Talk is a concise semantic notation system engineered for systems thinkers to map, compress, and scan information based on its underlying structural relationships. Instead of memorizing static vocabulary, meanings are derived compositionally by combining a Subject (1st symbol) with a State (2nd symbol). By learning just 9 core symbols, you can dynamically construct or decode all 81 foundational Semantic Primes on the fly—turning a page of notes into a scannable, multi-dimensional semantic map. Two Talk works natively in any plaintext environment, including text editors (Vim, Nano, Notepad++) and PKM systems (Obsidian, Notion, Logseq).

Two Talk is not a spoken language; it is a plaintext semantic notation system optimized for thought compression, rapid note-taking, and information density.

Examples

Journal: Life is good right now. :@ :! !!
PKM: Positive Thought ?>.!!
Todo: Review important notes before tomorrow. ?#.!! !> #> :< :>

Core Symbols

The entire Two Talk system is based on nine core symbols. Each symbol holds two distinct meanings depending on its structural position: as the 1st symbol (Subject), it defines the broad domain of experience; as the 2nd symbol (State), it applies a predictable, transformational modifier to that domain.

Semantic Primes

Every semantic prime below is the logical combination of its two component symbols. You do not need to memorize these pairs. To decode a prime, read the first character as the subject, and the second character as it's state. For example: ? (Mind) + ^ (Space/Anchor) combined to mean a localized mind state: learn / focus. Use this matrix to calculate your primitives on the fly. Cross-reference the 1st symbol (Subject Row) with the 2nd symbol (State Column).

Grammar

  1. Symbol Pairs: Words are made of two characters read left to right. The first symbol is the subject (main topic), and the second symbol is the state (change or description). ?^ = mind + anchor = learn
  2. Combining Words: Use a dot . to stack words together to create complex words. Read them left to right. Dots group words together before spaces do. #%.>^ = (thing + move) = vehicle / ?>.!!.:< = ((think + good) + past) = reflected decision
  3. Sentence Structure: Sentences follow the order: Who → What → Action. Use spaces between words, and use a new line (Enter key) to start a new sentence or clause. @! #% >> = I thing do.
  4. Opposites (Negation): Put <! before a word connected with a dot to say "not" or mean the opposite of something. <!.?! = not . want = dislike / don't want
  5. Numbers and Names: Use numbers for amounts (123), dates (2026-05-18), or times (1430). Use capital initials for names of people, parties, places, products, or projects (BM).

Dictionary

The Two Talk dictionary is includes into three distinct categories.

Semantic Primes form the logical foundation of Two Talk. These are universal concepts that cannot be broken down into simpler terms. By combining a Subject with a State, you "build" a primitive concept on the fly. For example, @ (Identity) modified by ! (Vital/Max) yields @! (I / Me).

Swadesh Stacked vocabulary builds directly on the Semantic Primes to express physical, real-world concepts. Rather than adding new characters, these are descriptive compounds joined by a dot separator. For instance, the physical body part "Head" is naturally constructed by stacking Body and Big: #@.%>.

Ogden Core terms optimize this exact same calculation system for general and professional contexts, mapping Ogden's 850 basic words to double-pair stacks. This establishes a fully expressive, highly compressed base vocabulary right out of the box while preserving the core semantic logic of the primes.

The Semantic Primes are canon in Two Talk and their meanings never change. The Swadesh Stacked and Ogden Core meanings are derived from those Semantic Primes. While these two layers provide an optimized, ready-to-use framework covering up to 80% of daily communication, they serve as established, high-frequency baselines that can be expanded or modified as specialized vocabularies require. Because the underlying geometric and semantic logic remains consistent, future readers can dynamically deduce or decode any compound meaning from context alone.

Learn

Learning Two Talk is fundamentally different from traditional, phonetic-based shorthand systems. Because it is engineered as a semantic notation system for systems thinkers, your speed does not come from mimicking the sound of spoken English. Instead, it comes from structural, character-level compression and the elimination of grammatical noise.

The Learning Curve: For beginners, Two Talk will feel slower at first. Writing a sentence requires a brief moment of active semantic translation - your brain must actively categorize a thought, find its foundational Subject row, and intersect it with a corresponding State.

However, once you cross this initial cognitive threshold, the system triggers a powerful shift into pattern recognition and muscle memory. Because the core vocabulary layers (the Semantic Primes, Swadesh list, and Ogden Core) are completely standardized, you will quickly stop calculating symbols on the fly and start typing them as intuitive geometric shapes.

At that point, you will gain a speed advantage because you are typing significantly fewer characters per sentence, dropping structural grammar clutter entirely, and keeping your fingers anchored to a tight, highly optimized cluster of core punctuation symbols and keys.

This tool will help you learn the Two Talk symbols and meanings to get started.
